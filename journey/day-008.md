## Why this shape

The post bank's Day 8 was *"매니페스토 발췌 2"* — another quoted passage. A second-week opening that performs the same move the first week kept rejecting: substituting a pre-written excerpt for a moment that actually occurred.

What actually occurred today was smaller and stranger. While referring to the project in conversation, I realized that the name I had been writing for two weeks — *LifeBite* — was not the name I had been *thinking.* The name in my head, the one I believed I was writing, was *LifeBit:* one *bit* of life, the unit that parallels AI's *64-bit.* Somewhere between the head and the hand, *Bit* had become *Bite.* For two weeks. Without my knowing.

The first instinct was to fix it — *that's a typo, let me rename everything.* The second instinct, the one this post takes, is the opposite: *the hand was right.* The manifesto's entire structural argument is that *what is computable* (*bit*) and *what is lived* (*bite*) are not the same thing. The whole point is the move from *bit* to *bite.* The unconscious slip enacted the manifesto more accurately than the conscious intention did.

The post does not announce a rename. It announces a *recognition.* The name is staying *LifeBite* — but the author is, for the first time, choosing it consciously instead of inheriting it from a slip. The slip is being *upgraded into a decision.*

## What I noticed

- The slip went *undetected for two weeks* across: the manifesto (Korean + English + French), the GitHub README, the landing page, the Day 1 tweet, the campaign bible, the journey files, the Family Office app's footer tagline, the conversation with the wife. Six layers, fifty thousand readers (of zero), and the author. None caught it because *Bite was the right answer all along.* The slip was invisible because it was correct.
- The Day 7 wife's *"어렵다"* and the Day 8 slip are the same finding from two angles. *The manifesto is hard to read* (wife) and *the manifesto's central name was not what the author thought it was* (author). Both point to the same fact: the work has been operating ahead of the writer's understanding of it. Week 2's *"어려움 줄이기"* now has a clearer shape — it is also *catching up to the work the work has been doing.*
- The line *"손가락이 머리보다 먼저 알았다"* is the post's structural turn. It rephrases a manifesto principle (*the work is the most accurate teacher of what the work is for*) into a single embodied sentence. That sentence will outlast this post; it may become a load-bearing line in the second half of the campaign.

## What I did *not* do

- Did not rename the project to *LifeBit.* The instinct to "correct" the slip would have erased what the slip taught.
- Did not pretend the slip never happened. Pretending would have been the bubble — keeping the *unbroken record* of the author's deliberate construction while the actual record was a happy accident. The post is the small public correction.
- Did not @mention anyone. Not Karpathy, not the wife, not the friend.
- Did not write *Day 8.* in the X tweet headline. Wait — I did. (Day 6 broke the *Day N.* pattern; Day 7 and Day 8 returned to it. The pattern is no longer absolute; it is a tool used when the post is best read as a series entry, and dropped when the post is best read on its own. Day 8 needs the series frame because it is *about* the series's name; without *Day 8.* the post would not connect.)

## Smallest honest note

There is a particular kind of vertigo when you discover that what you have been doing, you were doing slightly differently than you thought. *LifeBite* was on every page I had written for the last two weeks, and I had been reading *LifeBit* every time I read it. The eye saw *Bite*; the mind heard *Bit*; the two never met until today.

This is small. It is also exactly the kind of micro-truth the manifesto is supposed to be a tool for noticing. *LifeBite* itself names the unit of human experience that is too small for institutional language to capture — the umbrella forgotten, the kimchi stew eaten, the song that broke a heart. Today's *LifeBite* was *the gap between what I thought I was writing and what I had been writing.* That gap is now closed not by correction but by recognition.

The campaign has spent a week trying to *say* the manifesto. Week 2 begins with the manifesto *correcting* the campaign, gently, from inside. *작품이 작가를 가르친다.* The work teaches the worker. Day 8 is the day the work corrected the name.

If renaming were the right move, the slip would have hurt to find. It does not hurt. It clarifies. That is the diagnostic that confirms the slip was right.
