## Why this shape

The post bank's Day 7 was the *"첫 주 마감"* — a tally of likes, retweets, new followers, and Karpathy responses (presumed zero). A campaign that had been numbers-watching would have used this slot to count.

But Day 1 through Day 6 had already established that *measurements do not run the work,* and that the campaign's first-week test is internal, not external. The honest first-week measure was not a number but the answer to a question: *did anyone real read it.* On Day 7 the answer became *yes — the closest person.* And what she said was *"어렵다."*

This is the cleanest possible first-week result. Not because it is positive — it is not. Because it is *real.* A stranger saying *"great"* would have been less useful than someone close saying *"hard."* The campaign was set up to receive exactly this kind of return, and on Day 7 it arrived.

The post does three things in nine lines:

1. **Names the week's structural close** — *"Day 7. 1주차."*
2. **Reports the fact** — *와이프가 읽었다. 어렵다고 했다.*
3. **Names the meaning** — *"한 명이 와서 봤다. 어렵다는 정확한 피드백을 주고 갔다. 가장 가까운 한 사람이 가장 먼저 어려움을 말한 것 — 그게 1주차다."*

The final line — *그게 1주차다* — is the campaign's first explicit verdict on a week. It does not say *"successful."* It does not say *"failed."* It says *"that's what it was."* Naming-without-judging is the same move the wife made: *어렵다.*

---

## What I noticed

- The Day 3 friend said *"사차원이야."* The Day 7 wife said *"어렵다."* Both are honest, both come from closeness, and neither is praise. The pattern is real: *the people closest are the first to challenge.* This is not a problem to be solved — it is the campaign's safety system. If the closest readers were validating, the work would be performing.
- *"어렵다"* is a different *kind* of feedback than *"사차원이야"*. The friend's word was a *classification* (you are not like us). The wife's word is an *experience* (reading this is hard). Classifications are about the writer; experiences are about the reader. The campaign now has its first reader-experience datum, and it points to one direction for week 2.
- The X post chose to keep the headline on *"한 명"* rather than escalate to *"한 명이 — 게다가 와이프가."* The choice was structural: the universal *"한 명"* lets the post be read by anyone whose closest person is also their first reader. The relationship-specific framing arrives in the post's body, not its headline.

## What I did *not* do

- Did not post a numbers tally for week one. No likes, no retweets, no follower counts — and not because they don't exist, but because they were not the week's actual measurement.
- Did not soften the *"어렵다."* No *"그래도 의미는 있었다"* or *"읽어준 것만으로 감사하다."* Both would have been bubble — performance of gratitude over the actual fact of difficulty.
- Did not ask the wife *what specifically was hard* yet. That conversation belongs to a different setting, not the tweet. Asking it well takes its own preparation.
- Did not @mention anyone.

## Smallest honest note

When this campaign started, the test I imagined was: *will Karpathy notice.* On Day 7 the test changed shape. The real test, the one I had not named, was: *will the person closest to me be able to read this.*

She read it. She said it was hard. That is the most accurate feedback the manifesto has received in any form — because it came from someone who *will continue to be there next week* whether I act on the feedback or not. Friends drift, strangers click away, Karpathy does not arrive. The wife stays. Her *hard-to-read* is therefore a *persistent* hard-to-read, not a passing one.

The second week begins tomorrow, and the work is no longer *write more.* The work is *make it less hard to read.* The manifesto's structural argument — that *each thing belongs in its right place* (*각득기소*) — applies to the manifesto itself. Some sentences belong in the manifesto, and some belong in margin notes. Some belong in the writer's working memory and not the reader's first encounter. Sorting these is the labor of week two.

If the manifesto is going to be a tool other people can use, it first has to be a tool *one specific other person* can use. The wife is that person. Karpathy was always a direction; the wife is the destination.
